Differentiation of a Function from \( \mathbb{R}^n \) to \( \mathbb{R}^m \)

Let \( f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m \) be a function. The function \( f \) is said to be differentiable at a point \( \mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n \) if there exists a linear map \( Df(\mathbf{a}): \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m \) such that

\[ \lim_{\mathbf{h} \to \mathbf{0}} \frac{\| f(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{h}) - f(\mathbf{a}) - Df(\mathbf{a})(\mathbf{h}) \|}{\| \mathbf{h} \|} = 0. \]

The linear map \( Df(\mathbf{a}) \) is called the derivative or differential of \( f \) at \( \mathbf{a} \). It is often represented by the Jacobian matrix, which is an \( m \times n \) matrix given by

\[ J_f(\mathbf{a}) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{a}) & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_2}(\mathbf{a}) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{a}) \\ \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{a}) & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_2}(\mathbf{a}) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{a}) \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{a}) & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_2}(\mathbf{a}) & \cdots & \frac{\partial f_m}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{a}) \end{bmatrix}. \]

Examples

Example 1: Linear Transformation

Consider the function \( f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2 \) defined by \( f(x, y) = (2x, 3y) \). The Jacobian matrix of \( f \) is

\[ J_f(x, y) = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}. \]

Example 2: Non-linear Transformation

Consider the function \( g: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2 \) defined by \( g(x, y) = (x^2, \sin(y)) \). The Jacobian matrix of \( g \) is

\[ J_g(x, y) = \begin{bmatrix} 2x & 0 \\ 0 & \cos(y) \end{bmatrix}. \]
