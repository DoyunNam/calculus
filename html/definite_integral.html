The definite integral of a one-variable function \( f(x) \) over an interval \([a, b]\) is a fundamental concept in calculus that represents the signed area under the curve \( f(x) \) from \( x = a \) to \( x = b \). It is denoted as:

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx \]

Mathematically, the definite integral is defined as the limit of a Riemann sum as the number of subintervals approaches infinity. Specifically,

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = \lim_{{n \to \infty}} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*) \Delta x \]

where \( \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n} \) is the width of each subinterval, and \( x_i^* \) is a sample point in the \( i \)-th subinterval.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus links the definite integral to the antiderivative of the function. If \( F(x) \) is an antiderivative of \( f(x) \), then:

\[ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a) \]

This theorem provides a powerful method for evaluating definite integrals.
