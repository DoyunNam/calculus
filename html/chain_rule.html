Chain Rule of One Variable Functions

The chain rule in calculus is a formula for computing the derivative of the composition of two or more functions. If we have two functions \(f\) and \(g\), where \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\), the chain rule states:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \]

Proof of the Chain Rule

Let's denote \(y = f(u)\) and \(u = g(x)\). We want to find \(\frac{dy}{dx}\).

By definition of the derivative, we have:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{{\Delta x \to 0}} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} \]

Since \(y = f(g(x))\), let \(\Delta y = f(g(x + \Delta x)) - f(g(x))\).

We can rewrite \(\Delta y\) as:

\[ \Delta y = f(g(x + \Delta x)) - f(g(x)) = f(u + \Delta u) - f(u) \]

where \(\Delta u = g(x + \Delta x) - g(x)\).

Now, the difference quotient for \(f\) with respect to \(u\) is:

\[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} = \frac{f(u + \Delta u) - f(u)}{\Delta u} \]

and for \(g\) with respect to \(x\):

\[ \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} = \frac{g(x + \Delta x) - g(x)}{\Delta x} \]

Therefore,

\[ \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} \]

Taking the limit as \(\Delta x \to 0\), we get:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{{\Delta x \to 0}} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \lim_{{\Delta x \to 0}} \left( \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} \right) = \left( \lim_{{\Delta u \to 0}} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \right) \cdot \left( \lim_{{\Delta x \to 0}} \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} \right) \]

which simplifies to:

\[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx} \]
