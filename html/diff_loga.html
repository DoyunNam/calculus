Differentiation of Logarithmic Functions

The differentiation of logarithmic functions can be expressed as follows:

The derivative of the natural logarithm \( \ln(x) \): \[ \frac{d}{dx} \ln(x) = \frac{1}{x} \]

The derivative of the logarithm with base \(a\), \( \log_a(x) \): \[ \frac{d}{dx} \log_a(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln(a)} \]

Proofs

1. Derivative of \( \ln(x) \)

To prove \( \frac{d}{dx} \ln(x) = \frac{1}{x} \):

Let \( y = \ln(x) \). Then, by the definition of the natural logarithm: \[ e^y = x \] Differentiating both sides with respect to \( x \): \[ \frac{d}{dx} (e^y) = \frac{d}{dx} (x) \] Using the chain rule on the left side: \[ e^y \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \] Since \( e^y = x \): \[ x \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \] Solving for \( \frac{dy}{dx} \): \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x} \] Hence, \[ \frac{d}{dx} \ln(x) = \frac{1}{x} \]

2. Derivative of \( \log_a(x) \)

To prove \( \frac{d}{dx} \log_a(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln(a)} \):

Recall the change of base formula for logarithms: \[ \log_a(x) = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)} \] Let \( y = \log_a(x) \). Then: \[ y = \frac{\ln(x)}{\ln(a)} \] Differentiating both sides with respect to \( x \): \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\ln(a)} \frac{d}{dx} (\ln(x)) \] From the derivative of \( \ln(x) \): \[ \frac{d}{dx} (\ln(x)) = \frac{1}{x} \] Substituting this into the equation: \[ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\ln(a)} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \] Therefore, \[ \frac{d}{dx} \log_a(x) = \frac{1}{x \ln(a)} \]
