Integration of a Power Series

Consider a power series of the form:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n \]

We want to integrate this power series term-by-term. The integral of \( f(x) \) from 0 to \( x \) is:

\[ \int_0^x f(t) \, dt = \int_0^x \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n t^n \, dt \]

Assuming the power series converges uniformly, we can interchange the sum and the integral:

\[ \int_0^x \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n t^n \, dt = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \int_0^x a_n t^n \, dt \]

Integrating each term individually, we get:

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n \int_0^x t^n \, dt = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n \left[ \frac{t^{n+1}}{n+1} \right]_0^x \]

Evaluating the integral, we have:

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n \left( \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} \right) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{a_n}{n+1} x^{n+1} \]

Therefore, the integral of the power series \( f(x) \) is:

\[ \int_0^x f(t) \, dt = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{a_n}{n+1} x^{n+1} \]

Proof of Term-by-Term Integration

The key step in the integration process is the interchange of the sum and the integral. We need to justify this step. This justification is usually provided by the following theorem:

Theorem (Uniform Convergence): If a series of functions \( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} g_n(x) \) converges uniformly to a function \( g(x) \) on an interval \( [a, b] \), and if each \( g_n(x) \) is integrable, then

\[ \int_a^b \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} g_n(x) \, dx = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \int_a^b g_n(x) \, dx \]

In our case, \( g_n(t) = a_n t^n \), which are continuous (and thus integrable) on any closed interval \( [0, x] \). The power series \( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n t^n \) converges uniformly on \( [0, x] \) if \( |t| \leq R \) (where \( R \) is the radius of convergence). Therefore, we can interchange the sum and the integral.

This completes the proof of term-by-term integration of the power series.
