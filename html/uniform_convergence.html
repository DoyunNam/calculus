Uniform Convergence

A sequence of functions \( \{ f_n \} \) defined on a set \( A \) is said to converge uniformly to a function \( f \) if for every \( \epsilon > 0 \), there exists an integer \( N \) such that for all \( n \geq N \) and for all \( x \in A \), \[ | f_n(x) - f(x) | < \epsilon. \]

This means that the convergence of \( f_n \) to \( f \) happens at the same rate for all points \( x \in A \). Uniform convergence is stronger than pointwise convergence, which only requires \( f_n(x) \) to converge to \( f(x) \) for each individual \( x \in A \) as \( n \to \infty \).

Example 1

Consider the sequence of functions \( f_n(x) = x^n \) defined on the interval \([0, 1]\). For \( x \in [0, 1) \), \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} x^n = 0, \] and for \( x = 1 \), \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(1) = 1. \] Hence, \( f_n \) converges pointwise to the function \[ f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } x \in [0, 1) \\ 1 & \text{if } x = 1 \end{cases}. \] However, the convergence is not uniform because for any \( N \), we can find \( x \) close enough to 1 such that \( | f_n(x) - f(x) | \) is not small for all \( n \geq N \).

Example 2

Consider the sequence of functions \( f_n(x) = \frac{x}{n} \) defined on the interval \([0, 1]\). For all \( x \in [0, 1] \), \[ \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{x}{n} = 0. \] Hence, \( f_n \) converges pointwise to the zero function \( f(x) = 0 \). The convergence is also uniform because for any \( \epsilon > 0 \), we can choose \( N \) such that for all \( n \geq N \) and for all \( x \in [0, 1] \), \[ \left| \frac{x}{n} \right| < \epsilon. \]
