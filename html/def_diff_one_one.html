Differentiation of a Function from \( \mathbb{R} \) to \( \mathbb{R} \)

Differentiation is a concept in calculus that deals with the rate at which a function is changing at any given point. If \( f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R} \) is a function, its derivative, denoted by \( f'(x) \) or \( \frac{df}{dx} \), represents the rate of change of \( f \) with respect to \( x \). Formally, the derivative of \( f \) at \( x \) is defined as:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \]

This limit, if it exists, gives the slope of the tangent line to the graph of \( f \) at the point \( (x, f(x)) \).

Examples

1. Let \( f(x) = x^2 \). The derivative of \( f \) is:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = \lim_{{h \to 0}} (2x + h) = 2x \]

2. Let \( f(x) = \sin(x) \). The derivative of \( f \) is:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin(x)}{h} \]

Using the trigonometric identity for the sine of a sum, we get:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{\sin(x)\cos(h) + \cos(x)\sin(h) - \sin(x)}{h} = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \left( \sin(x) \frac{\cos(h) - 1}{h} + \cos(x) \frac{\sin(h)}{h} \right) \]

Knowing that \( \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{\cos(h) - 1}{h} = 0 \) and \( \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{\sin(h)}{h} = 1 \), we get:

\[ f'(x) = \cos(x) \]

3. Let \( f(x) = e^x \). The derivative of \( f \) is:

\[ f'(x) = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{e^{x+h} - e^x}{h} = \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{e^x e^h - e^x}{h} = e^x \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} \]

Since \( \lim_{{h \to 0}} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} = 1 \), we get:

\[ f'(x) = e^x \]
