Taylor Series for Multivariable Functions

Definition

The Taylor series of a function \( f \) at a point \( \mathbf{a} = (a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n) \) is given by:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{a}) + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a})(x_i - a_i) + \frac{1}{2!} \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}(\mathbf{a})(x_i - a_i)(x_j - a_j) + \cdots \]

In a more compact form, this can be written as:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{|\alpha| \geq 0} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha \]

where \( \alpha = (\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \ldots, \alpha_n) \) is a multi-index, \( |\alpha| = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 + \cdots + \alpha_n \), \( \alpha! = \alpha_1! \alpha_2! \cdots \alpha_n! \), \( D^\alpha = \frac{\partial^{|\alpha|}}{\partial x_1^{\alpha_1} \partial x_2^{\alpha_2} \cdots \partial x_n^{\alpha_n}} \), and \( (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha = (x_1 - a_1)^{\alpha_1} (x_2 - a_2)^{\alpha_2} \cdots (x_n - a_n)^{\alpha_n} \).

Proof

The proof of the Taylor series for a multivariable function follows similar lines to the single-variable case but requires handling partial derivatives and multi-index notation.

Consider a function \( f \) that is infinitely differentiable at \( \mathbf{a} \). We start with the first-order approximation:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) \approx f(\mathbf{a}) + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a})(x_i - a_i) \]

Including the second-order terms, we get:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) \approx f(\mathbf{a}) + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a})(x_i - a_i) + \frac{1}{2!} \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}(\mathbf{a})(x_i - a_i)(x_j - a_j) \]

By induction, including higher-order terms gives the general form:

\[ f(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{|\alpha| \geq 0} \frac{D^\alpha f(\mathbf{a})}{\alpha!} (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a})^\alpha \]

Examples

Example 1: Consider \( f(x, y) = e^{x+y} \). The Taylor series at \( (0,0) \) is:

\[ e^{x+y} = 1 + (x+y) + \frac{(x+y)^2}{2!} + \frac{(x+y)^3}{3!} + \cdots \]

Example 2: Consider \( f(x, y) = \sin(x) \cos(y) \). The Taylor series at \( (0,0) \) is:

\[ \sin(x)\cos(y) = xy - \frac{x^3y}{6} - \frac{xy^3}{6} + \frac{x^3y^3}{36} + \cdots \]
