Limit Superior

In mathematics, the limit superior (or lim sup) of a sequence \( \{a_n\} \) is defined as the largest limit point of the sequence. It is denoted as \( \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n \). Formally, it can be defined as:

\[ \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( \sup_{k \geq n} a_k \right). \]

This means that the limit superior is the limit of the sequence of supremums of the tail ends of the original sequence.

Properties of Limit Superior

  1. Bounded Sequences: If \( \{a_n\} \) is a bounded sequence, then \( \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n \) exists and is finite.
  2. Subsequence Limits: \( \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n \) is the largest value that is the limit of some subsequence of \( \{a_n\} \).
  3. Monotonic Sequences: If \( \{a_n\} \) is non-increasing, then \( \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \).

Proofs of Properties

1. Bounded Sequences

Let \( \{a_n\} \) be a bounded sequence. Then there exists some \( M \) such that \( |a_n| \leq M \) for all \( n \). By definition, \( \sup_{k \geq n} a_k \) is a decreasing sequence bounded below by \( -M \). Hence, its limit as \( n \to \infty \) exists and is finite.

2. Subsequence Limits

Let \( L = \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n \). By definition, for every \( \epsilon > 0 \), there exists \( N \) such that for all \( n \geq N \), we have \( L - \epsilon \leq \sup_{k \geq n} a_k \leq L + \epsilon \). This implies there exists a subsequence \( \{a_{n_k}\} \) such that \( a_{n_k} \to L \) as \( k \to \infty \). Thus, \( L \) is the limit of some subsequence of \( \{a_n\} \).

3. Monotonic Sequences

If \( \{a_n\} \) is non-increasing, then for all \( n \), \( \sup_{k \geq n} a_k = a_n \). Thus:

\[ \limsup_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left( \sup_{k \geq n} a_k \right) = \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n. \]
