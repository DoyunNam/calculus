Differentiation of a Power Series

Consider a power series of the form:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n \]

We want to find the derivative of this power series with respect to \(x\).

Step-by-Step Differentiation

To differentiate \( f(x) \), we differentiate term by term:

\[ f'(x) = \frac{d}{dx} \left( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n \right) \]

Using the linearity of differentiation and the power rule \(\frac{d}{dx} x^n = n x^{n-1}\), we get:

\[ f'(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{d}{dx} (a_n x^n) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n \frac{d}{dx} (x^n) \]

Applying the power rule to each term:

\[ f'(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n n x^{n-1} \]

We can reindex the summation by letting \(m = n - 1\). When \(n = 0\), the term vanishes because \(n x^{n-1}\) for \(n = 0\) is zero. Thus, we start the summation from \(n = 1\) and let \(m = n - 1\):

\[ f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n n x^{n-1} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m+1} (m+1) x^m \]

Therefore, the derivative of the power series is:

\[ f'(x) = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m+1} (m+1) x^m \]

Proof of Validity

The term-by-term differentiation of a power series is valid within the radius of convergence of the original series. Suppose the power series:

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n \]

has a radius of convergence \(R\). Within this radius, the series converges uniformly. Differentiation of uniformly convergent series can be performed term-by-term.

Thus, within the interval \(|x| < R\), we have:

\[ f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n n x^{n-1} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} a_{m+1} (m+1) x^m \]

This completes the proof.
