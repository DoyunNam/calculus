Sequence in Mathematics

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers that often follows a specific pattern or rule. Each number in the sequence is called a term. A sequence can be finite or infinite.

Definition

A sequence can be defined as a function from the set of natural numbers (or a subset of the natural numbers) to the real numbers.

Formally, a sequence is a function \(a: \mathbb{N} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), where \(a(n)\) is the \(n\)-th term of the sequence. We usually denote a sequence by \(\{a_n\}\).

Properties of Sequences

  • Convergence: A sequence \(\{a_n\}\) is said to converge to a limit \(L\) if for every \(\epsilon > 0\), there exists a positive integer \(N\) such that \(|a_n - L| < \epsilon\) for all \(n > N\).
  • Divergence: If a sequence does not converge, it is said to diverge.
  • Monotonicity: A sequence is monotonic if it is either increasing or decreasing.
  • Boundedness: A sequence is bounded if there exists a real number \(M\) such that \(|a_n| \leq M\) for all \(n \in \mathbb{N}\).

Example and Proof of Convergence

Consider the sequence \(\left\{ \frac{1}{n} \right\}\). We will prove that it converges to 0.

Proof:

Let \(\epsilon > 0\) be given. We need to find a positive integer \(N\) such that for all \(n > N\), \(\left| \frac{1}{n} - 0 \right| < \epsilon\).

Since \(\left| \frac{1}{n} - 0 \right| = \frac{1}{n}\), we need \(\frac{1}{n} < \epsilon\).

Taking the reciprocal, we get \(n > \frac{1}{\epsilon}\). Let \(N = \left\lceil \frac{1}{\epsilon} \right\rceil \geq \frac{1}{\epsilon} \) .

Then for all \(n > N\), \(\frac{1}{n} < \frac{1}{N} \leq \epsilon\).

Thus, \(\left\{ \frac{1}{n} \right\}\) converges to 0.
