Surface Integral

A surface integral is a generalization of multiple integrals to integration over surfaces. It can be thought of as the double integral analog of the line integral. In more rigorous terms, it is an integral where the function to be integrated is evaluated over a surface in three-dimensional space.

Mathematical Definition

Given a scalar function \( f(x, y, z) \) and a surface \( S \) parameterized by \( \mathbf{r}(u, v) \), the surface integral of \( f \) over \( S \) is given by:

\[ \iint_S f(x, y, z) \, dS = \iint_D f(\mathbf{r}(u, v)) \left\| \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v} \right\| du \, dv \]

For a vector field \( \mathbf{F} = (F_1, F_2, F_3) \), the surface integral over \( S \) is:

\[ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u, v)) \cdot \left( \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v} \right) du \, dv \]

Examples

Example 1: Surface Integral of a Constant Function

Consider the function \( f(x, y, z) = 1 \) over the surface of the unit sphere \( x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1 \). The parameterization of the sphere is:

\[ \mathbf{r}(\theta, \phi) = (\sin \phi \cos \theta, \sin \phi \sin \theta, \cos \phi) \]

The surface integral is:

\[ \iint_S 1 \, dS = \iint_0^\pi \iint_0^{2\pi} (\sin \phi) \, d\theta \, d\phi \]

Evaluating the integral:

\[ \iint_S 1 \, dS = \iint_0^\pi \iint_0^{2\pi} \sin \phi \, d\theta \, d\phi = 4\pi \]

Example 2: Surface Integral of a Simple Vector Field

Consider the vector field \( \mathbf{F} = (0, 0, 1) \) over the unit disk in the \( xy \)-plane, \( x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \). The parameterization of the disk is:

\[ \mathbf{r}(r, \theta) = (r \cos \theta, r \sin \theta, 0) \]

The surface integral is:

\[ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_0^1 \iint_0^{2\pi} (0, 0, 1) \cdot (r \cos \theta, r \sin \theta, 0) \, r \, d\theta \, dr \]

Since the dot product is zero, the surface integral evaluates to zero:

\[ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0 \]
