Green's Theorem

Statement: Green's Theorem relates a line integral around a simple closed curve \( C \) to a double integral over the plane region \( D \) bounded by \( C \). The theorem is stated as follows:

\[ \oint_C (P \, dx + Q \, dy) = \iint_D \left( \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right) \, dA \]

Proof:

The proof of Green's Theorem can be divided into two parts, considering the components \( P \, dx \) and \( Q \, dy \) separately. We start with the component \( P \, dx \). Assume that the orientation of a curve \( C \) is counterclockwise.

1. Proof for \( \oint_C P \, dx \):

Consider a region \( D \) in the plane bounded by the simple closed curve \( C \). We can divide \( D \) into many small rectangles by drawing lines parallel to the \( x \)- and \( y \)-axes.

For a small rectangle with sides of length \( \Delta x \) and \( \Delta y \), the contribution to the line integral \( \oint_C P \, dx \) is the sum of the values of \( P \) along the top and bottom edges of the rectangle:

\[ - \oint_{\text{rectangle}} P \, dx = P(x, y+\Delta y) \Delta x - P(x, y) \Delta x = \left( P(x, y+\Delta y) - P(x, y) \right) \Delta x \]

As \( \Delta y \to 0 \), this becomes:

\[ - \oint_{\text{rectangle}} P \, dx = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \Delta x \Delta y \]

Summing over all such rectangles in \( D \), we get:

\[ \oint_C P \, dx = - \iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \, dA \]

2. Proof for \( \oint_C Q \, dy \):

Similarly, consider the component \( Q \, dy \). For a small rectangle, the contribution to the line integral \( \oint_C Q \, dy \) is the sum of the values of \( Q \) along the left and right edges of the rectangle:

\[ \oint_{\text{rectangle}} Q \, dy = Q(x+\Delta x, y) \Delta y - Q(x, y) \Delta y = \left( Q(x+\Delta x, y) - Q(x, y) \right) \Delta y \]

As \( \Delta x \to 0 \), this becomes:

\[ \oint_{\text{rectangle}} Q \, dy = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} \Delta x \Delta y \]

Summing over all such rectangles in \( D \), we get:

\[ \oint_C Q \, dy = \iint_D \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} \, dA \]

Combining both parts, we have:

\[ \oint_C (P \, dx + Q \, dy) = \iint_D \left( \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right) \, dA \]

Thus, Green's Theorem is proved.

Examples:

Example 1:

Let \( P = -y \) and \( Q = x \). Calculate \( \oint_C (-y \, dx + x \, dy) \) where \( C \) is the unit circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \).

Using Green's Theorem:

\[ \oint_C (-y \, dx + x \, dy) = \iint_D \left( \frac{\partial x}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial (-y)}{\partial y} \right) \, dA = \iint_D (1 + 1) \, dA = 2 \iint_D \, dA \]

Since \( D \) is the unit disk, \( \iint_D \, dA = \pi \), so:

\[ \oint_C (-y \, dx + x \, dy) = 2\pi \]

Example 2:

Let \( P = 2xy \) and \( Q = x^2 \). Calculate \( \oint_C (2xy \, dx + x^2 \, dy) \) where \( C \) is the triangle with vertices at \( (0,0) \), \( (1,0) \), and \( (0,1) \).

Using Green's Theorem:

\[ \oint_C (2xy \, dx + x^2 \, dy) = \iint_D \left( \frac{\partial x^2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial (2xy)}{\partial y} \right) \, dA = \iint_D (2x - 2x) \, dA = \iint_D 0 \, dA = 0 \]

Thus:

\[ \oint_C (2xy \, dx + x^2 \, dy) = 0 \]
