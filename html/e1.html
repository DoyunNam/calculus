Defining \( e \) in Mathematics

Definition 1: Limit Definition

The number \( e \) can be defined as the limit:

\[ e = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \]

Definition 2: Series Definition

The number \( e \) can also be defined by the infinite series:

\[ e = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \]

Definition 3: Differential Equation Definition

The number \( e \) is the unique number such that the function \( f(x) = e^x \) satisfies the differential equation:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x \]

Equivalence of Definitions

Equivalence of Limit and Series Definitions

First, we show that the series definition converges to the limit definition. Consider the series:

\[ e = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \]

We can write:

\[ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^k \]

As \( n \to \infty \), the terms of the binomial expansion approximate the terms of the series definition of \( e \), showing that the limit definition converges to the same value as the series definition.

Equivalence of Series and Differential Equation Definitions

We show that the series definition satisfies the differential equation. Consider:

\[ e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} \]

Differentiating term-by-term, we get:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} e^x = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{nx^{n-1}}{n!} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^m}{m!} = e^x \]

where we let \( m = n-1 \). This shows that \( e^x \) satisfies the differential equation, proving the equivalence of these definitions.

Conclusion

The number \( e \) can be equivalently defined by the limit, series, and differential equation definitions, and we have shown their equivalence through mathematical proofs.
