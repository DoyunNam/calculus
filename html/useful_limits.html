Useful Limits and Their Proofs

1. \( \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h} = 1 \)

Proof:

Consider the unit circle and the angle \( h \) (in radians). The length of the arc subtended by \( h \) is \( h \) itself. The sine of the angle \( h \) is the length of the perpendicular from the point on the circle to the x-axis. From \[ \sin h \leq h \leq \tan h \]

we have the inequality:

\[ \cos h \leq \frac{\sin h}{h} \leq 1 \]

As \( h \) approaches 0, \( \cos h \) approaches 1. Thus, by the Squeeze Theorem:

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h} = 1 \]

2. \( \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} = 1 \)

Proof:

The derivative of \( e^x \) at \( x = 0 \) is defined as:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} e^x \bigg|_{x=0} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^{h} - e^0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} \]

Since the derivative of \( e^x \) is \( e^x \), and \( e^0 = 1 \), we get:

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{e^h - 1}{h} = 1 \]

3. \( \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+h)}{h} = 1 \)

Proof:

The derivative of \( \ln(x) \) at \( x = 1 \) is defined as:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} \ln(x) \bigg|_{x=1} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+h) - \ln(1)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+h)}{h} \]

Since the derivative of \( \ln(x) \) is \( \frac{1}{x} \), and \( \frac{1}{1} = 1 \), we get:

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+h)}{h} = 1 \]

4. \( \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1+h)^k - 1}{h} = k \)

Proof:

The derivative of \( (1+x)^k \) at \( x = 0 \) is defined as:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} (1+x)^k \bigg|_{x=0} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1+h)^k - (1+0)^k}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1+h)^k - 1}{h} \]

Using the binomial theorem, we can expand \( (1+h)^k \) around \( h = 0 \) to get:

\[ (1+h)^k \approx 1 + kh \]

Thus, the limit becomes:

\[ \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1+h)^k - 1}{h} = k \]
