Exponential Functions

An exponential function is a mathematical function of the form:

$$ f(x) = a \cdot b^x $$

where:

  • \( a \) is a constant called the initial value or the coefficient.
  • \( b \) is the base of the exponential function.
  • \( x \) is the exponent.

For the function to be an exponential function, the base \( b \) must be a positive real number not equal to 1. The function grows or decays at a rate proportional to its current value.

Examples of exponential functions include:

  • Growth: \( f(x) = 2^x \)
  • Decay: \( f(x) = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^x \)

In applications, exponential functions are often used to model phenomena such as population growth, radioactive decay, and interest compounding in finance.
