Line Integral

A line integral (also known as a path integral) is an integral where the function to be integrated is evaluated along a curve. The curve is defined by a parametrization, and the integral sums up the function values along this curve. There are two main types of line integrals:

  • Scalar Line Integrals: Integrate a scalar function along a curve.
  • Vector Line Integrals: Integrate a vector field along a curve.

Scalar Line Integral

The scalar line integral of a function \( f \) over a curve \( C \) parametrized by \( \mathbf{r}(t) \) from \( t = a \) to \( t = b \) is given by:

\[ \int_C f(\mathbf{r}) \, ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \|\mathbf{r}'(t)\| \, dt \]

Vector Line Integral

The vector line integral of a vector field \( \mathbf{F} \) along a curve \( C \) is given by:

\[ \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) \, dt \]

Examples

Example 1: Scalar Line Integral

Consider the function \( f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 \) and the curve \( C \) which is the unit circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \) parametrized by \( \mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t) \) for \( t \) in \([0, 2\pi]\). The scalar line integral is:

\[ \int_C (x^2 + y^2) \, ds = \int_0^{2\pi} (\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t) \|\mathbf{r}'(t)\| \, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 1 \cdot 1 \, dt = 2\pi \]

Example 2: Vector Line Integral

Consider the vector field \( \mathbf{F}(x, y) = (y, -x) \) and the curve \( C \) which is the unit circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \) parametrized by \( \mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t) \) for \( t \) in \([0, 2\pi]\). The vector line integral is:

\[ \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} ( \sin t, -\cos t) \cdot (-\sin t, \cos t) \, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} ( -\sin^2 t - \cos^2 t) \, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} -1 \, dt = -2\pi \]

Physical Meaning of Line Integral

A line integral is a type of integral where a function is evaluated along a curve. It generalizes the concept of integrating over an interval to integrating over a curve or path in space. The physical meaning of a line integral depends on the context and the function being integrated.

Example 1: Work Done by a Force Field

In physics, one common use of line integrals is to calculate the work done by a force field \( \mathbf{F} \) on a particle moving along a path \( C \). The work \( W \) is given by the line integral:

\[ W = \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} \]

Here, \( \mathbf{F} \) is the force field, \( d\mathbf{r} \) is a differential element of the path \( C \), and \( \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} \) represents the dot product of the force vector and the displacement vector. This integral sums up the work done by the force along each infinitesimal segment of the path.

Example 2: Circulation of a Vector Field

Another application of line integrals is in fluid dynamics, where it is used to compute the circulation of a vector field \( \mathbf{V} \) around a closed curve \( C \). The circulation \( \Gamma \) is given by:

\[ \Gamma = \oint_C \mathbf{V} \cdot d\mathbf{r} \]

Here, \( \mathbf{V} \) is the velocity field of the fluid, and the integral sums the dot product of the velocity and the path's tangent vector around the closed curve, representing the total circulation around the curve.

Example 3: Flux Across a Curve

Line integrals can also be used to calculate the flux of a vector field \( \mathbf{F} \) across a curve \( C \). The flux \( \Phi \) is given by:

\[ \Phi = \int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} \, ds \]

In this case, \( \mathbf{n} \) is the unit normal vector to the curve \( C \), and \( ds \) is a differential element of the arc length. This integral measures how much of the field \( \mathbf{F} \) passes through the curve \( C \).

Line integrals are powerful tools in various fields of science and engineering, providing a way to sum quantities along curves in space.
