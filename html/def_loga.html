Logarithmic Functions

A logarithmic function is the inverse of an exponential function. The logarithm of a number is the exponent to which the base must be raised to produce that number. The general form of a logarithmic function is:

\[ y = \log_b(x) \]

where:

  • \(y\) is the logarithm of \(x\) to the base \(b\).
  • \(b\) is the base of the logarithm.
  • \(x\) is the argument of the logarithm.

The logarithmic function can be rewritten in its exponential form:

\[ b^y = x \]

Common bases used in logarithmic functions include:

  • Base 10 (common logarithm), written as \(\log_{10}(x)\) or simply \(\log(x)\).
  • Base \(e\) (natural logarithm), written as \(\log_e(x)\) or \(\ln(x)\), where \(e \approx 2.71828\).

Some important properties of logarithms include:

  • \(\log_b(1) = 0\) because \(b^0 = 1\).
  • \(\log_b(b) = 1\) because \(b^1 = b\).
  • \(\log_b(xy) = \log_b(x) + \log_b(y)\) (Product Rule).
  • \(\log_b\left(\frac{x}{y}\right) = \log_b(x) - \log_b(y)\) (Quotient Rule).
  • \(\log_b(x^k) = k \log_b(x)\) (Power Rule).
  • \(\log_b(x) = \frac{\log_k(x)}{\log_k(b)}\) (Change of Base Formula).
