Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus consists of two parts:

Part 1

If \( f \) is continuous on the interval \([a, b]\) and \( F \) is an antiderivative of \( f \) on \([a, b]\), then

\[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a). \]

Part 2

If \( f \) is a continuous real-valued function defined on a closed interval \([a, b]\), and

\[ F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt, \]

then \( F \) is continuous on \([a, b]\), differentiable on \((a, b)\), and \( F'(x) = f(x) \) for all \( x \) in \((a, b)\).

Proof of Part 1

Let \( F \) be an antiderivative of \( f \), meaning \( F'(x) = f(x) \). By the definition of the definite integral as the limit of Riemann sums, we have

\[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*) \Delta x, \]

where \(\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}\) and \( x_i^* \) is a sample point in the \( i \)-th subinterval.

Using the Mean Value Theorem for integrals, there exists a point \( c_i \) in the \( i \)-th subinterval such that

\[ f(c_i) \Delta x = F(x_{i+1}) - F(x_i). \]

Summing over all subintervals, we get

\[ \sum_{i=1}^n f(c_i) \Delta x = \sum_{i=1}^n [F(x_{i+1}) - F(x_i)] = F(b) - F(a). \]

Taking the limit as \( n \to \infty \), we have

\[ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a). \]

Proof of Part 2

Define \( F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt \). We need to show that \( F'(x) = f(x) \).

By the definition of the derivative,

\[ F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{F(x+h) - F(x)}{h}. \]

Using the definition of \( F(x) \), we have

\[ F(x+h) = \int_a^{x+h} f(t) \, dt \quad \text{and} \quad F(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt. \]

Therefore,

\[ F(x+h) - F(x) = \int_a^{x+h} f(t) \, dt - \int_a^x f(t) \, dt = \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt. \]

Hence,

\[ F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt. \]

By the Mean Value Theorem for integrals, there exists a point \( c \) in \([x, x+h]\) such that

\[ \int_x^{x+h} f(t) \, dt = f(c) \cdot h. \]

Thus,

\[ F'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(c) \cdot h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} f(c) = f(x). \]

This completes the proof of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
