Defining \(e\) and Proving Equivalences

The mathematical constant \(e\) can be defined in several equivalent ways. Here are some of the most common definitions along with proofs of their equivalence:

1. Limit Definition

\[ e = \lim_{{n \to \infty}} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \]

2. Series Definition

\[ e = \sum_{{n=0}}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \]

3. Differential Equation Definition

The number \(e\) is the unique number such that the function \(f(x) = e^x\) satisfies:

\[ \frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x \quad \text{and} \quad e^0 = 1 \]

Proofs of Equivalence

1. Limit and Series

Consider the limit definition: \[ e = \lim_{{n \to \infty}} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \] Using the binomial theorem, expand \(\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n\): \[ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = \sum_{{k=0}}^n \binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^k \] For large \(n\), this approximates: \[ \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n \approx \sum_{{k=0}}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k!} \] Therefore, as \(n \to \infty\), the series converges to the series definition of \(e\): \[ e = \sum_{{n=0}}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \]

2. Series and Differential Equation

Consider the series definition: \[ e^x = \sum_{{n=0}}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} \] Differentiate term by term: \[ \frac{d}{dx} \left( \sum_{{n=0}}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} \right) = \sum_{{n=1}}^{\infty} \frac{n x^{n-1}}{n!} = \sum_{{n=1}}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} = \sum_{{m=0}}^{\infty} \frac{x^m}{m!} = e^x \] Therefore, \[ \frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x \quad \text{and} \quad e^0 = 1 \]

Thus, we have shown that the different definitions of \(e\) are equivalent.
