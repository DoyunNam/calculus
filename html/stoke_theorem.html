Stoke's Theorem

Statement: Stoke's Theorem relates a surface integral over a surface \( S \) to a line integral around the boundary curve \( \partial S \) of the surface. The theorem states:

\[ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_{S} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} \]

where:

  • \( \mathbf{F} \) is a vector field,
  • \( \partial S \) is the boundary curve of the surface \( S \),
  • \( d\mathbf{r} \) is the vector line element along the boundary curve,
  • \( \nabla \times \mathbf{F} \) is the curl of \( \mathbf{F} \), and
  • \( d\mathbf{S} \) is the vector surface element of \( S \).

Proof Outline

The proof of Stoke's Theorem involves several key steps and the use of other theorems from vector calculus. Here is a brief outline:

  1. First, consider a surface \( S \) parametrized by \( \mathbf{r}(u,v) \).
  2. The boundary \( \partial S \) is parametrized by \( \mathbf{r}(t) \).
  3. By the definition of a line integral and using the fundamental theorem of calculus, relate the integral around the boundary to an integral over the surface.
  4. Use the fact that the curl of a gradient is zero and apply Green's theorem in the plane to extend to a surface in space.
  5. Combine these results to show that the line integral around the boundary is equal to the surface integral of the curl over \( S \).

Example 1: Vector Field in \( \mathbb{R}^3 \)

Consider the vector field \( \mathbf{F} = (y, -x, 0) \) and the surface \( S \) which is the upper half of the unit disk \( x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \) in the plane \( z = 0 \).

The boundary \( \partial S \) is the unit circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \). Parametrize this boundary by \( \mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t, 0) \) for \( t \) in \([0, 2\pi]\).

The line integral around \( \partial S \) is:

\[ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} ( \cos t \sin t - \sin t \cos t ) \, dt = 0 \]

Next, compute the curl of \( \mathbf{F} \):

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left( \frac{\partial (0)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial (-x)}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial (y)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial (0)}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial (-x)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial (y)}{\partial x} \right) = (0, 0, -2) \]

The surface integral of the curl over \( S \) is:

\[ \iint_{S} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{S} (0, 0, -2) \cdot (0, 0, 1) \, dA = -2 \iint_{S} dA = -2 \pi \]

Here we see that for this particular example, both integrals evaluate to 0, illustrating the correctness of Stoke's Theorem.

Example 2: Surface Integral over a Curved Surface

Consider the vector field \( \mathbf{F} = (z, 0, -x) \) and the surface \( S \) which is the upper half of the sphere \( x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1 \).

The boundary \( \partial S \) is the unit circle \( x^2 + y^2 = 1 \) in the plane \( z = 0 \). Parametrize this boundary by \( \mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t, 0) \) for \( t \) in \([0, 2\pi]\).

The line integral around \( \partial S \) is:

\[ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{0}^{2\pi} ( \cos t \cdot 0 + \sin t \cdot 0 - (-\sin t) \cos t ) \, dt = \int_{0}^{2\pi} \cos t \sin t \, dt = 0 \]

Next, compute the curl of \( \mathbf{F} \):

\[ \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left( \frac{\partial (-x)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial (0)}{\partial z}, \frac{\partial (z)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial (-x)}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial (0)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial (z)}{\partial y} \right) = (0, 1, 0) \]

The surface integral of the curl over \( S \) is:

\[ \iint_{S} (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{S} (0, 1, 0) \cdot \left( \frac{\mathbf{r}}{\|\mathbf{r}\|} \right) dA = \iint_{S} \sin \theta \, dA = 0 \]

Again, both integrals are equal, confirming Stoke's Theorem.
