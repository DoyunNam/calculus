Convergence Tests for Series

1. The n-th Term Test for Divergence

If \( \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \neq 0 \), then the series \( \sum a_n \) diverges.

Example: Consider the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \). Since \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0 \), the n-th term test does not conclude divergence.

2. The Geometric Series Test

A geometric series \( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n \) converges if \( |r| < 1 \) and diverges if \( |r| \geq 1 \).

Example: Consider the series \( \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n \). Since \( \left| \frac{1}{2} \right| < 1 \), the series converges.

3. The p-Series Test

A p-series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} \) converges if \( p > 1 \) and diverges if \( p \leq 1 \).

Example: Consider the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \). Since \( p = 2 > 1 \), the series converges.

Proof

Proof of the p-Series Test

The p-series is given by \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} \).

Case 1: \( p > 1 \)

To prove convergence for \( p > 1 \), we use the integral test. Consider the function \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x^p} \). This function is positive, continuous, and decreasing for \( x \geq 1 \).

We need to evaluate the improper integral:

\[ \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, dx \]

Let's find the antiderivative of \( \frac{1}{x^p} \) for \( p \neq 1 \):

\[ \int \frac{1}{x^p} \, dx = \int x^{-p} \, dx = \frac{x^{-p+1}}{-p+1} + C = \frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p} + C \]

Now, evaluate the improper integral:

\[ \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, dx = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_{1}^{b} \frac{1}{x^p} \, dx = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p} \right]_{1}^{b} \]

Compute the definite integral:

\[ = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left( \frac{b^{1-p}}{1-p} - \frac{1^{1-p}}{1-p} \right) = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left( \frac{b^{1-p}}{1-p} - \frac{1}{1-p} \right) \]

Since \( p > 1 \), we have \( 1 - p < 0 \), which means \( b^{1-p} \to 0 \) as \( b \to \infty \):

\[ = \left( 0 - \frac{1}{1-p} \right) = \frac{1}{p-1} \]

Thus, the integral converges and so does the p-series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} \) for \( p > 1 \).

Case 2: \( p \leq 1 \)

To prove divergence for \( p \leq 1 \), we again use the integral test. Consider the function \( f(x) = \frac{1}{x^p} \) with \( p \leq 1 \). We need to evaluate the improper integral:

\[ \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, dx \]

We already have the antiderivative \( \frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p} \). Now, evaluate the improper integral for \( p = 1 \):

\[ \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \lim_{b \to \infty} \int_{1}^{b} \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \lim_{b \to \infty} [\ln x]_{1}^{b} = \lim_{b \to \infty} (\ln b - \ln 1) = \lim_{b \to \infty} \ln b = \infty \]

Thus, the integral diverges for \( p = 1 \), so the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \) diverges.

For \( p < 1 \), the evaluation is:

\[ \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \, dx = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left[ \frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p} \right]_{1}^{b} = \lim_{b \to \infty} \left( \frac{b^{1-p}}{1-p} - \frac{1}{1-p} \right) \]

Since \( p < 1 \), we have \( 1 - p > 0 \), which means \( b^{1-p} \to \infty \) as \( b \to \infty \):

\[ = \infty \]

Thus, the integral diverges and so does the p-series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} \) for \( p \leq 1 \).

4. The Integral Test

If \( f(x) \) is a positive, continuous, and decreasing function for \( x \geq 1 \), then the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) and the integral \( \int_{1}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx \) either both converge or both diverge.

Example: Consider the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \). The corresponding integral is \( \int_{1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{x^2} \, dx = 1 \), which converges, so the series converges.

5. The Comparison Test

Given two series \( \sum a_n \) and \( \sum b_n \) with \( 0 \leq a_n \leq b_n \) for all \( n \), if \( \sum b_n \) converges, then \( \sum a_n \) converges.

Example: Consider \( a_n = \frac{1}{n^2+1} \) and \( b_n = \frac{1}{n^2} \). Since \( \sum \frac{1}{n^2} \) converges and \( \frac{1}{n^2+1} \leq \frac{1}{n^2} \), the series \( \sum \frac{1}{n^2+1} \) converges by comparison.

6. The Limit Comparison Test

Given two series \( \sum a_n \) and \( \sum b_n \) with \( a_n > 0 \) and \( b_n > 0 \) for all \( n \), if \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = c \) where \( c \) is a positive finite number, then \( \sum a_n \) converges if and only if \( \sum b_n \) converges.

Example: Consider \( a_n = \frac{n+1}{n^2} \) and \( b_n = \frac{1}{n} \). Since \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n+1}{n^2} \cdot n = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n+1}{n} = 1 \), and \( \sum \frac{1}{n} \) diverges, so does \( \sum \frac{n+1}{n^2} \).

7. The Ratio Test

For a series \( \sum a_n \), if \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| = L \), then the series converges if \( L < 1 \) and diverges if \( L > 1 \). If \( L = 1 \), the test is inconclusive.

Example: Consider the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \). The ratio test gives \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{\frac{1}{(n+1)!}}{\frac{1}{n!}} \right| = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n+1} = 0 \), so the series converges.

8. The Root Test

For a series \( \sum a_n \), if \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = L \), then the series converges if \( L < 1 \) and diverges if \( L > 1 \). If \( L = 1 \), the test is inconclusive.

Example: Consider the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left( \frac{1}{2^n} \right) \). The root test gives \( \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{\left| \frac{1}{2^n} \right|} = \frac{1}{2} < 1 \), so the series converges.

9. Root Test with Limsup and Proof

Let \( \{a_n\} \) be a sequence of real numbers. The root test is used to determine the convergence or divergence of the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \). The test is based on the n-th root of the absolute value of the terms of the sequence.

Define the n-th root test as follows:

Let \( L = \limsup_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} \).

Then:

  • If \( L < 1 \), the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) converges absolutely.
  • If \( L > 1 \), the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) diverges.
  • If \( L = 1 \), the root test is inconclusive.

Proof

Let \( L = \limsup_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} \).

Case 1: \( L < 1 \)

Since \( L < 1 \), there exists \( 0 < r < 1 \) such that \( L < r < 1 \). By the definition of \(\limsup\), there exists an integer \( N \) such that for all \( n \geq N \), \( \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} < r \). Therefore, \( |a_n| < r^n \) for all \( n \geq N \).

Consider the tail of the series \( \sum_{n=N}^{\infty} |a_n| \). Since \( |a_n| < r^n \) for all \( n \geq N \), we can compare it to the geometric series \( \sum_{n=N}^{\infty} r^n \).

The geometric series \( \sum_{n=N}^{\infty} r^n \) converges because \( 0 < r < 1 \). Hence, by the comparison test, the series \( \sum_{n=N}^{\infty} |a_n| \) converges. Adding the finite number of terms \( |a_1|, |a_2|, \ldots, |a_{N-1}| \), we conclude that the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |a_n| \) converges absolutely.

Case 2: \( L > 1 \)

Since \( L > 1 \), there exists \( r > 1 \) such that \( r < L \). By a property of \(\limsup\), there exists a subsequence of \( \{\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} \} \) whose limit is \( L \). Thus for some tail of \( |a_{n_k}| \) is greater than \( 1 \). Thus, the series \( \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \) diverges.

Case 3: \( L = 1 \)

If \( L = 1 \), the root test is inconclusive. The series may converge or diverge, and further analysis or a different convergence test is needed.
